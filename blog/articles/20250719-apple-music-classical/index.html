Apple Music Classical Still Doesn’t Understand Classical Music

Apple Music Classical is a welcome upgrade after 25 years of iTunes and Apple Music. I’ve spent countless hours wrangling metadata to shoehorn classical works into the album–track model. Apple’s new app feels like a breath of fresh air, but it isn’t flawless. Here are three ways Apple Music Classical still lets classical listeners down.

Siri arbitrarily orders movements

Driving home from a conducting workshop, I asked Siri to “play Brahms’s Third Symphony.”

To its credit, Siri got the basics right: Brahms’s Symphony No. 3 on Apple Music Classical, not the regular Apple Music. No "Can’t find that" nonsense.

The result? Baffling. Even while driving, I had to snap a screenshot:

The playback started with III and was to be followed by II, IV, and I. If I ask for a work, start at the beginning. If I want a specific movement, I will say so.

Apple Music Classical editors disregard works…

Recently, I dove into Dvořák. I know the "New World" Symphony well, but little else. I found this playlist:

The playlist includes only three of the four movements, scattered and from different performances. Multi-movement works demand to be heard whole. You can listen to a single movement—but deeper appreciation comes from the arc of the entire work.

…and also performances

The movements from Dvořák’s Ninth come from different performances—different conductors with different orchestras from a narrow five-year time window.

A conductor interprets an entire work. Bernstein’s Shostakovich Fifth—especially the finale—was a watershed interpretation that even Shostakovich preferred. Comparing performances is one of classical music's great pleasures, but it's undercut by Apple Music Classical’s editors.

Elsewhere, Apple Music Classical nails this. Its design surfaces a work’s variety of performances with a clear editorial perspective.

Here, editors spotlight a quintessential recording, foreground the diversity of performances, and provide context for the symphony’s reception and debates over interpretations. That’s excellent curation.

Conclusion

Classical music can intimidate new listeners. Its work–movement structure feels foreign in the age of streaming singles, and the jargon doesn’t help. Apple Music Classical is uniquely positioned to change that. If Apple truly cares about the genre—guiding new listeners while earning connoisseurs’ trust—they must shoulder these editorial responsibilities.
